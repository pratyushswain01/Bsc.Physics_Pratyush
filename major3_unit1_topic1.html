Electric Field, Flux & Gauss's Law

This topic explores the fundamental concepts of electrostatics, beginning with the definition of the electric field, leading to the concept of electric flux, and culminating in Gauss's Law, a powerful tool for calculating electric fields in situations with high symmetry.

1. The Electric Field ($\vec{E}$)

The Electric Field is a physical vector field that permeates space. At any given point, the electric field vector, $\vec{E}$, represents the electrostatic force, $\vec{F}$, that would be exerted on a stationary positive test charge, $q_0$, divided by the magnitude of that charge.

The formal definition uses a limit to ensure the test charge is infinitesimally small, so it doesn't disturb the original charge distribution creating the field.

$$ \vec{E} \equiv \lim_{q_0 \to 0} \frac{\vec{F}}{q_0} $$

From Coulomb's Law, the electric field generated by a single point charge $q$ at a distance $r$ is:

$$ \vec{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2} \hat{r} $$

Here, $\epsilon_0$ is the permittivity of free space ($\approx 8.854 \times 10^{-12}$ F/m) and $\hat{r}$ is a dimensionless unit vector pointing from the source charge towards the point of interest.

The Principle of Superposition

For a system of multiple charges, the net electric field at a point is the vector sum of the fields produced by each individual charge. For a system of N discrete charges:

$$ \vec{E}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \vec{E}_i = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{q_i}{r_i^2} \hat{r}_i $$

For continuous charge distributions (line, surface, or volume), we integrate the contributions from each infinitesimal charge element $dq$:

$$ \vec{E}(\vec{r}) = \int \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{dq}{r'^2} \hat{r}' $$

2. Electric Flux ($\Phi_E$)

Electric Flux is a scalar quantity that represents the measure of the "flow" or penetration of the electric field through a given surface. For a small, flat surface element $d\vec{A}$ in an electric field $\vec{E}$, the differential flux is the scalar product:

$$ d\Phi_E = \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = E \, dA \cos\theta $$

To find the total flux through a finite surface $S$, we integrate this expression over the entire surface:

$$ \Phi_E = \int_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} $$

3. Gauss's Law

Gauss's Law provides a profound relationship between the net electric charge enclosed within a surface and the net electric flux passing through that surface. It is one of Maxwell's four fundamental equations of electromagnetism.

Integral Form

Gauss's Law states that the net electric flux through any hypothetical closed surface (a "Gaussian surface") is equal to the net charge enclosed ($Q_{\text{enc}}$) within that surface, divided by the permittivity of free space ($\epsilon_0$).

$$ \oint_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\epsilon_0} $$

Differential Form

Using the Divergence Theorem from vector calculus, the integral form can be converted to a local, differential form. This form relates the divergence of the electric field at a point to the volume charge density $\rho$ at that same point.

$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} $$

This powerful equation shows that electric charges are the sources and sinks of the electric field.

4. Applications of Gauss's Law

Gauss's Law is incredibly useful for calculating the electric field for charge distributions with a high degree of symmetry. The strategy is to choose a Gaussian surface that matches the symmetry, allowing $E$ to be treated as a constant on the surface.

Example 1: Field of an Infinite Line of Charge

For an infinitely long wire with uniform linear charge density $\lambda$, we use a cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius $r$ and length $L$. The resulting electric field is:

$$ E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\epsilon_0 r} $$

Example 2: Field of an Infinite Plane of Charge

For an infinite plane with uniform surface charge density $\sigma$, we use a cylindrical "pillbox" as a Gaussian surface. The field is constant and independent of the distance from the plane:

$$ E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0} $$

Example 3: Field of a Uniformly Charged Sphere

For a non-conducting sphere of radius $R$ and total charge $Q$:

  • Outside the sphere ($r > R$): The field is identical to that of a point charge $Q$ at the center.
    • $$ E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{r^2} $$
  • Inside the sphere ($r < R$): The field increases linearly from the center.
    • $$ E = \frac{Qr}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R^3} $$
