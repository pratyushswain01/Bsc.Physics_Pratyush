Electrostatic Energy & Conductors

This section explores two critical aspects of electrostatics: the energy required to assemble a configuration of charges, which is stored in the resulting electric field, and the unique behavior of conducting materials when placed in an electrostatic environment.

1. Electrostatic Energy

The electrostatic potential energy of a system of charges is the work required to assemble the system by bringing the charges in from an infinite distance apart. Since the electrostatic force is conservative, this work is stored as potential energy.

Energy of a System of Point Charges

For a system of N discrete charges, the potential energy $W$ is the sum of the potential energies of each pair of charges. To avoid double-counting, a factor of 1/2 is introduced:

$$ W = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{j \neq i}^{N} \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_i q_j}{r_{ij}} $$

This can be expressed more conveniently in terms of the potential $V(\vec{r}_i)$ at the position of charge $q_i$ due to all other charges:

$$ W = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{N} q_i V(\vec{r}_i) $$

Energy of a Continuous Charge Distribution

For a continuous charge distribution with volume charge density $\rho$ and potential $V$, the summation becomes an integral over the volume $\tau$:

$$ W = \frac{1}{2} \int_\tau \rho(\vec{r}) V(\vec{r}) d\tau $$

Energy Stored in the Electric Field

A profound insight is that the electrostatic energy is not stored in the charges themselves, but in the electric field they create throughout space. Using vector calculus and the differential form of Gauss's law, the energy expression can be rewritten in terms of the electric field $\vec{E}$:

$$ W = \frac{\epsilon_0}{2} \int_{\text{all space}} E^2 d\tau $$

From this, we can define the electrostatic **energy density** ($u_E$), which is the energy per unit volume stored in the electric field:

$$ u_E = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_0 E^2 $$

This tells us that energy resides wherever an electric field exists.

2. Conductors in Electrostatic Equilibrium

A conductor contains a vast number of free charges (usually electrons) that can move throughout the material. When a conductor is in **electrostatic equilibrium**, there is no net motion of charge within it. This state leads to several key properties:

  • The electric field is zero inside a conductor. If there were an internal field, the free charges would move under its influence, violating the condition of equilibrium. The charges rearrange themselves on the surface to perfectly cancel any external field inside.
  • The net charge density is zero inside a conductor. From Gauss's law, $\nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \rho/\epsilon_0$. Since $\vec{E}=0$ inside, the charge density $\rho$ must also be zero.
  • Any net charge on a conductor resides entirely on its surface. This is a direct consequence of the previous two points.
  • The potential is constant throughout a conductor. Since $\vec{E} = -\nabla V$ and $\vec{E}=0$ inside, the potential $V$ cannot change from point to point. A conductor in equilibrium is an equipotential volume.
  • The electric field just outside a conductor is perpendicular to its surface. If there were a tangential component to the field, it would exert a force on surface charges and cause them to move, again violating equilibrium.

Electric Field at the Surface of a Conductor

Using Gauss's Law with a small cylindrical "pillbox" Gaussian surface piercing the conductor's surface, we can find a direct relationship between the surface charge density $\sigma$ and the magnitude of the electric field just outside:

$$ E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} $$

Capacitance

Capacitance ($C$) is a measure of a conductor's ability to store electric charge for a given potential. It is defined as the ratio of the charge $Q$ on the conductor to its potential $V$ (relative to a reference potential of zero at infinity).

$$ C = \frac{Q}{V} $$

The unit of capacitance is the Farad (F), where 1 Farad = 1 Coulomb/Volt. For an isolated spherical conductor of radius $R$, the capacitance is:

$$ C = 4\pi\epsilon_0 R $$
