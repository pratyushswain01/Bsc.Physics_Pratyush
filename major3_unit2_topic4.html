Ballistic Galvanometer

A ballistic galvanometer is a type of sensitive galvanometer used for measuring the total charge that passes through it in a short time interval, such as the discharge of a capacitor. Unlike a standard galvanometer which measures steady current, a ballistic galvanometer is designed to measure an impulsive current.

1. Principle and Construction

The core of a ballistic galvanometer is a moving coil suspended in a magnetic field. Key design features include:

  • Large Moment of Inertia ($I_m$): The coil is designed to have a large moment of inertia so that the passage of a brief current pulse gives it an angular impulse without the coil moving significantly during the pulse.
  • Weak Damping: The damping forces (like air resistance) are minimized so that the coil can swing freely after the impulse.
  • Long Time Period of Oscillation ($T$): A long period ensures that the current pulse is over long before the coil completes any significant part of its swing.

The instrument works by converting the initial angular impulse from the charge pulse into the kinetic energy of a torsional pendulum. This kinetic energy is then converted into potential energy at the peak of its swing, and the maximum deflection (the "throw," $\theta_0$) is measured.

2. Theory of Operation

The derivation relates the total charge $Q$ to the first maximum throw $\theta_0$ of the coil.

Impulsive Torque

If a transient current $i(t)$ flows through the coil for a short time, it produces a torque $\tau = N A B i(t)$, where $N$ is the number of turns, $A$ is the coil area, and $B$ is the magnetic field. The total angular impulse given to the coil is:

$$ \text{Angular Impulse} = \int \tau dt = \int (N A B i) dt = N A B \int i dt $$

Since the total charge is $Q = \int i dt$, the impulse is $NABQ$. This impulse imparts an initial angular velocity $\omega_0$ to the coil:

$$ N A B Q = I_m \omega_0 $$

Torsional Oscillation

After receiving the impulse, the coil swings. Its initial kinetic energy is converted into potential energy stored in the suspension fiber. The restoring torque of the fiber is $\tau_{res} = -C\theta$, where $C$ is the torsional constant.

By conservation of energy, the initial kinetic energy equals the maximum potential energy:

$$ \frac{1}{2} I_m \omega_0^2 = \frac{1}{2} C \theta_0^2 $$

Relating Charge to Throw

From the two equations above, we can express $\omega_0$ in two ways. Rearranging them gives $\omega_0 = \frac{NABQ}{I_m}$ and $\omega_0 = \theta_0 \sqrt{\frac{C}{I_m}}$. Equating these gives:

$$ \frac{NABQ}{I_m} = \theta_0 \sqrt{\frac{C}{I_m}} $$

Solving for the charge $Q$ gives:

$$ Q = \frac{I_m}{NAB} \theta_0 \sqrt{\frac{C}{I_m}} = \frac{\theta_0}{NAB} \sqrt{I_m C} $$

We know the time period of a torsional pendulum is given by $T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{I_m}{C}}$. From this, we can write $\sqrt{I_m C} = \frac{TC}{2\pi}$. Substituting this into our equation for charge:

$$ Q = \frac{\theta_0}{NAB} \left( \frac{TC}{2\pi} \right) = \left(\frac{C}{NAB}\right) \left(\frac{T}{2\pi}\right) \theta_0 $$

This is the fundamental equation for an undamped ballistic galvanometer. It shows that the charge is directly proportional to the first throw: $Q = k \theta_0$, where $k = \frac{C T}{2\pi N A B}$ is the ballistic constant or charge sensitivity of the galvanometer.

3. Damping and Correction

In a real galvanometer, damping is always present. The amplitude of oscillations decreases exponentially. For accurate measurements, a correction factor is applied. The corrected relationship is:

$$ Q = k \theta_0 \left(1 + \frac{\lambda}{2}\right) $$

Where $\lambda$ is the logarithmic decrement, a measure of the damping, determined by observing the ratio of successive swings.
