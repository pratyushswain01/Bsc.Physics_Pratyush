Network Theorems (Superposition, etc.)

Beyond Thevenin's and Norton's theorems, other powerful principles exist to simplify the analysis of complex linear circuits. This section covers the superposition theorem and the maximum power transfer theorem, both of which are essential tools in circuit analysis.

1. Superposition Theorem

The superposition theorem is particularly useful for circuits containing multiple independent sources (both voltage and current). It states that the total current through (or voltage across) any element in a linear circuit is the algebraic sum of the currents (or voltages) produced by each independent source acting alone.

Steps to Apply the Superposition Theorem:

  1. Select one independent source. Turn off all other independent sources in the circuit.
    • To turn off a voltage source, replace it with a short circuit (a wire with zero resistance).
    • To turn off a current source, replace it with an open circuit (a break in the wire).
  2. Calculate the desired current or voltage. Analyze the simplified circuit to find the contribution to the current or voltage from the single active source. Pay attention to the direction of currents and polarity of voltages.
  3. Repeat for all sources. Repeat steps 1 and 2 for each independent source in the original circuit, one at a time.
  4. Sum the results. Algebraically add the individual contributions calculated in the previous steps to find the total current or voltage in the original circuit.

Important Note: The superposition theorem applies only to linear quantities like voltage and current. It cannot be used to find power, because power ($P = I^2R$) is not a linear function of current.

2. Maximum Power Transfer Theorem

This theorem is crucial for designing systems where the goal is to deliver the most power possible from a source to a load, such as in audio amplifiers or communication systems. It states that, for a DC voltage source with an internal resistance ($R_s$), maximum power is delivered to a load resistor ($R_L$) when the load resistance is equal to the source resistance.

$$ R_L = R_s $$

More generally, using Thevenin's theorem, any complex linear network can be reduced to a Thevenin equivalent circuit ($V_{th}$ and $R_{th}$). The maximum power transfer theorem then states that maximum power will be delivered to a load resistor $R_L$ when:

$$ R_L = R_{th} $$

Under this condition, the maximum power delivered to the load is:

$$ P_{max} = \frac{V_{th}^2}{4 R_{th}} $$

When the load is matched to the source resistance, exactly half of the total power supplied by the source is dissipated in the source's internal resistance, and the other half is delivered to the load. This corresponds to an efficiency of 50%, which is the maximum achievable for power transfer, though not necessarily for energy efficiency.
