Q2. Carrying 02 Marks

a) Define dielectric strength of a dielectric.

Dielectric strength is the maximum electric field an insulating material can withstand without suffering electrical breakdown and losing its insulating properties. If the applied field exceeds this intrinsic value, the material becomes conductive. It is typically measured in units of Volts per meter (V/m) or Kilovolts per millimeter (kV/mm).

b) How is breaking potential difference related to dielectric strength?

The breaking potential difference (Vb), also known as breakdown voltage, is the maximum voltage that can be applied across a specific thickness of a dielectric material before it breaks down. It is directly proportional to the material's dielectric strength (Emax) and its thickness (d). The relationship is given by the formula: Vb = Emax × d.

c) Define polarization of dielectric materials.

Polarization (P) is the net electric dipole moment induced per unit volume within a dielectric material when subjected to an external electric field. This occurs as the field causes a slight separation of positive and negative charges within the atoms. For linear isotropic dielectrics, polarization is proportional to the electric field: P = χe ε0 E.

d) What is an electrical displacement vector?

The electrical displacement vector (D) is a field used in electromagnetism that accounts for the effect of free charges in a dielectric medium. It is defined as D = ε0E + P, combining the electric field (E) and the material's polarization (P). Its advantage is that its flux depends only on the free charges enclosed in a surface (Gauss's Law: ∮D·dA = Qfree).

e) Write differential and integral forms of Gauss theorem for a dielectric.

Gauss's theorem for a dielectric relates the electric displacement vector D to the distribution of free electric charge (ρfree).
• Integral Form: The total flux of D out of a closed surface S is equal to the total free charge Qfree enclosed within that surface: ∮D ⋅ dS = Qfree.
• Differential Form: The divergence of D at any point is equal to the volume density of free charge at that point: ∇ ⋅ D = ρfree.

f) Find the energy density of an electric field in a dielectric.

The energy density (u) represents the amount of energy stored in an electric field per unit volume. In a dielectric medium with permittivity ε, the energy density is given by the expression: u = ½ εE2. It can also be written in terms of the displacement vector D and electric field E as u = ½ D ⋅ E.

g) State Lenz's law.

Lenz's law states that the direction of the electromotive force (EMF) and the resulting induced current in a conductor is such that it creates a magnetic field that opposes the change in magnetic flux which produced it. This law, responsible for the negative sign in Faraday's law of induction, upholds the principle of conservation of energy.

h) State the 2nd law of Maxwell's equation.

Maxwell's 2nd law is Gauss's law for magnetism. It states that the net magnetic flux through any closed surface is always zero. Its mathematical forms are ∮B ⋅ dS = 0 (integral) and ∇ ⋅ B = 0 (differential). This law signifies that isolated magnetic poles (monopoles) do not exist.

i) What is the 'Curie's point'?

The Curie point, or Curie temperature (Tc), is the critical temperature above which a ferromagnetic material loses its permanent magnetic properties and becomes paramagnetic. At this temperature, thermal agitation becomes strong enough to overcome the long-range ordering of magnetic domains, destroying spontaneous magnetization.

j) Which is better for permanent magnets: steel or soft iron? Why?

Steel is better for permanent magnets. This is because its B-H hysteresis loop is wide, indicating it has high retentivity (retains strong magnetism) and high coercivity (resists demagnetization). Soft iron has a narrow loop, making it easy to magnetize and demagnetize, which is ideal for temporary magnets like electromagnets.

k) What is hysteresis? What does its area represent?

Hysteresis, in magnetism, is the lagging of the magnetic flux density (B) behind the magnetizing field (H) as a ferromagnetic material is taken through a cycle of magnetization. The area enclosed by the B-H hysteresis loop represents the **energy lost as heat per unit volume** of the material during one complete magnetization cycle.

l) Define reciprocity theorem of mutual inductances.

The reciprocity theorem for mutual inductance states that for any two stationary coils, the mutual inductance of the first coil with respect to the second coil (M12) is equal to the mutual inductance of the second coil with respect to the first (M21). This symmetric relationship is expressed as: M = M12 = M21.

m) Define Lorentz force.

The Lorentz force is the total electromagnetic force (F) on a charged particle q moving with velocity v in an electric field E and a magnetic field B. It is the vector sum of the electric force and the magnetic force, given by the equation: F = q(E + v × B).

n) Define resonance for an electrical network.

Electrical resonance is a condition in an AC circuit with both inductance (L) and capacitance (C) where the inductive reactance (XL = ωL) becomes equal to the capacitive reactance (XC = 1/ωC). At the resonant frequency, the circuit's impedance is minimal (in series) or maximal (in parallel) and becomes purely resistive.

o) Define half power points.

The half-power points are the two frequencies (ω1 and ω2), on either side of the resonant frequency, at which the power dissipated in a resonant circuit is exactly half of the maximum power dissipated at resonance. At these frequencies, the current drops to Imax / √2 of its peak value.

p) What is a rejector electric circuit?

A parallel resonant LCR circuit is called a rejector circuit. At its resonant frequency, the circuit offers maximum impedance to the current flow. Because it heavily impedes or "rejects" the current at this specific frequency while allowing other frequencies to pass more easily, it functions as a band-stop filter.

q) What is sharpness of resonance.

Sharpness of resonance describes the selectivity of a resonant circuit. A sharp resonance indicates that the resonance curve is narrow and steep, meaning the circuit responds strongly only to a very limited range of frequencies near the resonant frequency. This selectivity is quantitatively measured by the circuit's **Quality Factor (Q-factor)**.

r) Explain Q-factor as an amplification factor.

In a series LCR circuit at resonance, the voltage across the inductor (VL) and the capacitor (VC) can be significantly larger than the applied source voltage (Vin). The Q-factor serves as the voltage amplification factor in this context, where the relationship is VL = VC = Q × Vin.

s) Distinguish between ideal voltage and current source.

An **ideal voltage source** maintains a constant voltage across its terminals regardless of the current it delivers, and it has zero internal resistance. In contrast, an **ideal current source** supplies a constant current regardless of the voltage across its terminals, and it has infinite internal resistance.

t) Define Thevenin's voltage (VTh).

Thevenin's voltage (VTh) is the open-circuit potential difference measured across two output terminals of a linear network after the load has been removed. It represents the effective voltage of the entire source network as seen by the load and is a key component of the Thevenin equivalent circuit.

Q5. Carrying Five Marks

a) State and explain superposition theorem with analytical explanation.

Superposition Theorem

Statement: In any linear, bilateral network containing two or more independent sources, the response (current or voltage) in any element is the algebraic sum of the responses caused by each independent source acting alone, while all other independent sources are deactivated (replaced by their internal resistances).

Analytical Explanation & Procedure:

  1. Identify Linearity: The theorem applies only to linear circuits (where Ohm's law holds) and for calculating linear quantities like voltage and current. It cannot be directly used for power (P=I²R).
  2. Select One Source: Choose one independent source to keep active in the circuit.
  3. Deactivate Other Sources: All other independent sources must be deactivated. A voltage source is deactivated by replacing it with a short circuit (0V). A current source is deactivated by replacing it with an open circuit (0A). Dependent sources are always kept active.
  4. Calculate Partial Response: With only one source active, calculate the current or voltage (the "response") in the element of interest. Carefully note its magnitude and direction (or polarity).
  5. Repeat for All Sources: Repeat steps 2-4 for each independent source in the network, calculating the partial response from each one.
  6. Algebraic Summation: The final, total response in the element is the algebraic sum of all the partial responses calculated. It is crucial to be consistent with signs (e.g., current flowing down is positive, up is negative).

b) What is the procedure to convert a circuit to its Norton's equivalent?

Procedure for Norton's Equivalent Circuit

Norton's theorem simplifies a complex linear network into an equivalent circuit with a single current source (IN) in parallel with a single resistor (RN).

Steps:

  1. Find Norton Resistance (RN): First, remove the load resistor. Deactivate all independent sources (short voltage sources, open current sources). Calculate the equivalent resistance looking into the open terminals. This resistance is RN (Note: RN is always equal to RTh).
  2. Find Norton Current (IN): Return to the original circuit. Place a short circuit across the output terminals (where the load was). Calculate the current flowing through this short circuit. This short-circuit current is the Norton Current, IN.
  3. Construct the Equivalent Circuit: Draw the Norton equivalent circuit by placing the current source IN in parallel with the resistor RN. The load can then be reconnected to this simplified circuit for analysis.

c) State and explain B-H curve and hysteresis.

B-H Curve and Hysteresis

The B-H curve shows the relationship between magnetic flux density (B) and the applied magnetizing field (H) for a ferromagnetic material. For such materials, this relationship is non-linear and exhibits **hysteresis**, which is the lagging of B behind H.

Explanation of the Loop:

  1. Initial Magnetization: Starting with an unmagnetized sample (H=0, B=0), increasing H causes B to increase along the initial curve until it reaches saturation.
  2. Saturation: At saturation, nearly all magnetic domains are aligned, and a further increase in H produces very little increase in B.
  3. Retentivity: When H is reduced to zero, B does not return to zero. The remaining magnetism is called retentivity or remanence (Br).
  4. Coercivity: To bring B to zero, a magnetizing field must be applied in the opposite direction. The value of this field is the coercivity (Hc).
  5. Reverse Saturation: As H is made more negative, the material saturates in the opposite direction.
  6. Completing the Loop: Reducing the negative H back to zero and then increasing it in the positive direction again will trace the lower part of the curve, completing the loop. The area inside this loop represents the energy lost as heat per unit volume per cycle.

This "back EMF," according to Lenz's Law, opposes the very change in current that produced it. This tendency of a coil to resist changes in current is quantified by its inductance (L).

g) State and explain Faraday's laws of electromagnetism. Derive Faraday's law from motional EMF.

Statement of Faraday's Laws

1. First Law (Qualitative): An electromotive force (EMF) is induced in a closed electrical circuit whenever the magnetic flux passing through the circuit changes.

2. Second Law (Quantitative): The magnitude of the induced EMF is directly proportional to the time rate of change of the magnetic flux. This is mathematically expressed as:

ε = -
dΦBdt

Derivation using Motional EMF

Consider a conducting rod PQ of length l sliding with constant velocity v on two parallel rails, forming a closed loop in a uniform magnetic field B directed into the page.

  1. A free charge q inside the rod experiences a magnetic Lorentz force Fm = q(v × B), with a magnitude of Fm = qvB directed from Q to P.
  2. This force acts like a battery. The work done moving the charge from Q to P is W = Fml = qvBl.
  3. The induced EMF (motional EMF) is the work done per unit charge: ε = W/q = Blv.
  4. Now, consider the change in flux. In time dt, the rod moves a distance dx = v dt. The change in the loop's area is dA = l dx = l(v dt).
  5. The corresponding change in magnetic flux is dΦB = B dA = Blv dt.
  6. The time rate of change of flux is:
    dΦBdt
    = Blv
  7. Comparing steps 3 and 6, we see |ε| = |dΦB/dt|. Adding the negative sign from Lenz's Law gives the final law.
