Lorentz Force & Biot-Savart Law

This section introduces magnetostatics: the study of magnetic fields produced by steady currents. We will explore the force exerted by a magnetic field on moving charges and currents (the Lorentz Force) and the fundamental law for calculating the magnetic field from a current source (the Biot-Savart Law).

1. The Lorentz Force

A magnetic field, $\vec{B}$, exerts a force on a moving charge. This magnetic force is given by:

$$ \vec{F}_m = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B}) $$

Here, $q$ is the charge, $\vec{v}$ is its velocity, and $\vec{B}$ is the magnetic field vector. The direction of the force is given by the right-hand rule and is always perpendicular to both the velocity and the magnetic field. A key consequence is that the magnetic force does no work on the charge, as the force is always perpendicular to the displacement.

The Full Lorentz Force Law

If both electric ($\vec{E}$) and magnetic ($\vec{B}$) fields are present, the total force on a charge is the vector sum of the electric and magnetic forces. This is the complete Lorentz Force Law:

$$ \vec{F} = q\vec{E} + q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B}) = q(\vec{E} + \vec{v} \times \vec{B}) $$

Force on a Current-Carrying Wire

A current is a collection of moving charges. The total magnetic force on a wire carrying a steady current $I$ is found by integrating the force on each infinitesimal charge element:

$$ \vec{F}_m = \int (I d\vec{l}) \times \vec{B} $$

For a straight wire of length $L$ in a uniform field $\vec{B}$, this simplifies to:

$$ \vec{F}_m = I (\vec{L} \times \vec{B}) $$

2. The Biot-Savart Law

The Biot-Savart Law is the fundamental law of magnetostatics, analogous to Coulomb's Law in electrostatics. It allows us to calculate the magnetic field produced by a steady current. The law states that the differential magnetic field $d\vec{B}$ produced by an infinitesimal current element $I d\vec{l}$ at a point located by the position vector $\vec{r}$ is:

$$ d\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{I (d\vec{l} \times \hat{r})}{r^2} $$

Where $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space ($\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}$ TÂ·m/A), and $\hat{r}$ is the unit vector pointing from the current element to the point of interest. To find the total magnetic field, one must integrate this expression over the entire current distribution.

3. Applications of the Biot-Savart Law

Magnetic Field of a Long, Straight Wire

By integrating the Biot-Savart Law for an infinitely long, straight wire carrying current $I$, the magnitude of the magnetic field at a perpendicular distance $s$ from the wire is:

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi s} $$

The magnetic field lines form concentric circles around the wire, with the direction given by the right-hand rule.

Magnetic Field on the Axis of a Circular Loop

For a circular loop of radius $R$ carrying current $I$, the magnetic field at a point on the axis at a distance $z$ from the center is:

$$ B_z = \frac{\mu_0 I R^2}{2(R^2 + z^2)^{3/2}} $$

At the very center of the loop ($z=0$), this simplifies to:

$$ B_{\text{center}} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2R} $$
