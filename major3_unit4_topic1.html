AC Circuits & LCR Circuits

This topic explores the behavior of circuits driven by an alternating current (AC) source. Unlike direct current (DC), AC voltage and current vary sinusoidally with time, leading to complex behaviors in circuits containing inductors and capacitors.

1. AC and Phasors

An AC voltage source is typically described by:

$$ V(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t) $$

where $V_0$ is the peak voltage (amplitude) and $\omega$ is the angular frequency. To analyze AC circuits, we use **phasors**, which are vectors that rotate in a complex plane with angular frequency $\omega$. The projection of the phasor onto the imaginary axis represents the instantaneous value of the voltage or current. This technique converts differential equations into algebraic ones.

2. Reactance and Impedance

In AC circuits, inductors and capacitors exhibit a form of opposition to current flow called **reactance**.

  • Inductive Reactance ($X_L$): The opposition from an inductor. The voltage across an inductor leads the current by $90^\circ$ (or $\pi/2$ radians). Its reactance is: $X_L = \omega L$.
  • Capacitive Reactance ($X_C$): The opposition from a capacitor. The voltage across a capacitor lags the current by $90^\circ$. Its reactance is: $X_C = \frac{1}{\omega C}$.

The total opposition to current flow in an AC circuit, including both resistance and reactance, is called **impedance ($Z$)**. It is the AC equivalent of resistance. The peak voltage, peak current ($I_0$), and impedance are related by Ohm's Law for AC circuits: $V_0 = I_0 Z$.

3. The Series LCR Circuit

A series LCR circuit contains a resistor ($R$), an inductor ($L$), and a capacitor ($C$) connected in series with an AC source. The total impedance is the vector sum of the resistance and the total reactance.

Impedance of a Series LCR Circuit

Since the inductive and capacitive reactances are $180^\circ$ out of phase, they subtract. The total impedance $Z$ is found using the Pythagorean theorem on the impedance phasor diagram:

$$ Z = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2} = \sqrt{R^2 + \left(\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega C}\right)^2} $$

Phase Angle

The total voltage across the circuit is out of phase with the current by an angle $\phi$, given by:

$$ \tan\phi = \frac{X_L - X_C}{R} = \frac{\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega C}}{R} $$
  • If $X_L > X_C$, the circuit is inductive, and the voltage leads the current ($\phi > 0$).
  • If $X_C > X_L$, the circuit is capacitive, and the voltage lags the current ($\phi < 0$).

4. Resonance in LCR Circuits

A special condition called **resonance** occurs when the inductive reactance equals the capacitive reactance ($X_L = X_C$). At this point, the impedance is at its minimum value ($Z=R$), and the current is maximum. The phase angle $\phi$ is zero, meaning the voltage and current are in phase.

The resonant angular frequency, $\omega_0$, is found by setting $X_L=X_C$:

$$ \omega_0 L = \frac{1}{\omega_0 C} \implies \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} $$

Resonance is a key principle in tuning circuits, used in radio receivers and many other applications to select a specific frequency.
