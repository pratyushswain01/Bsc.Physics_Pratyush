Ampere's Law & Applications

While the Biot-Savart law is universal, it can be difficult to compute. Ampere's Law provides a simpler method for calculating the magnetic field in situations with a high degree of symmetry. It is the magnetostatic equivalent of Gauss's Law in electrostatics.

1. Ampere's Circuital Law

Ampere's Law relates the line integral of the magnetic field around a closed loop (called an Amperian loop) to the total steady current passing through the surface enclosed by that loop.

Integral Form

The law is stated mathematically as:

$$ \oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}} $$

Where $\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l}$ is the line integral of the magnetic field around a closed path, and $I_{\text{enc}}$ is the net current piercing the surface bounded by the path. The power of this law lies in choosing an Amperian loop that matches the symmetry of the problem, making the integral easy to evaluate.

Differential Form

Using Stokes' Theorem from vector calculus, which states $\oint \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{F}) \cdot d\vec{A}$, we can express Ampere's law in a local, differential form. The enclosed current can also be written as an integral of the current density $\vec{J}$.

$$ \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{B}) \cdot d\vec{A} = \mu_0 \int_S \vec{J} \cdot d\vec{A} $$

Since this must hold for any surface, the integrands must be equal. This gives the differential form of Ampere's Law:

$$ \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J} $$

This equation states that steady currents are the source of curl in the magnetic field. A non-zero curl at a point signifies a circulation of the field, which is what magnetic field lines do around a current.

2. Applications of Ampere's Law

Field of a Long, Straight Wire

For an infinitely long wire with current $I$, we choose a circular Amperian loop of radius $r$ centered on the wire. By symmetry, $B$ is constant in magnitude on this loop and parallel to $d\vec{l}$.

$$ \oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = B \oint dl = B(2\pi r) $$

Applying Ampere's law: $B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I$. This gives the familiar result:

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} $$

Field of a Long Solenoid

A solenoid is a coil of wire wound into a long helix. For an ideal, infinitely long solenoid with $n$ turns per unit length carrying current $I$, the magnetic field inside is uniform and strong, while the field outside is negligible.

We choose a rectangular Amperian loop with one side of length $L$ inside the solenoid and the parallel side outside. The only contribution to the integral comes from the side inside the solenoid.

$$ \oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = BL $$

The enclosed current is $I_{\text{enc}} = nLI$. Applying Ampere's Law: $BL = \mu_0(nLI)$. This yields the uniform field inside:

$$ B = \mu_0 n I $$

Field of a Toroid

A toroid is a solenoid bent into a donut shape. For a toroid with $N$ total turns carrying current $I$, we use a circular Amperian loop of radius $r$ inside the windings.

$$ \oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 N I $$

The magnetic field inside the toroid is confined within its core and is given by:

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 N I}{2\pi r} $$
