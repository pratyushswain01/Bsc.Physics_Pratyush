Method of Images

The Method of Images is a powerful and elegant problem-solving technique in electrostatics used to solve boundary-value problems. Instead of directly solving Laplace's or Poisson's equation, which can be mathematically intensive, this method replaces complex boundary conditions (like the surface of a conductor) with a simpler configuration of imaginary "image charges."

1. The Core Idea

The method is based on the Uniqueness Theorem. If we can find a configuration of charges that satisfies the given boundary conditions, then the potential and field in the region of interest are uniquely determined by that configuration.

The steps are as follows:

  1. Identify the real charges and the conductive boundaries.
  2. Remove the boundaries and strategically place one or more imaginary "image charges" in the regions where the solution is *not* required (e.g., inside the conductor).
  3. Adjust the position and magnitude of these image charges until the potential they create (along with the original charges) matches the known potential on the original boundary surfaces.
  4. The potential in the original region of interest is now simply the superposition of potentials from the real charges and the newly placed image charges.

2. Point Charge and a Grounded Conducting Plane

This is the classic example of the method. Consider a point charge $+q$ held at a distance $d$ from an infinite, grounded ($V=0$) conducting plane.

The Setup

We want to find the potential $V$ in the space above the plane. We replace the conducting plane with a single image charge. To satisfy the boundary condition $V=0$ on the plane, the image charge must be:

  • Of opposite sign: $-q$
  • Located at the same perpendicular distance $d$ behind the plane.

Calculating the Potential

The problem is now reduced to finding the potential of two point charges, $+q$ and $-q$. For any point $(x, y, z)$ in the region of interest (where the plane is at $z=0$ and the charge is at $(0,0,d)$), the potential is:

$$ V(x,y,z) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \left( \frac{q}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + (z-d)^2}} - \frac{q}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + (z+d)^2}} \right) $$

This solution is valid for the region $z \geq 0$. The potential is zero for $z < 0$.

Induced Surface Charge Density

The presence of the charge $+q$ induces a negative charge on the surface of the conductor. We can find this charge density, $\sigma$, using the relation $\sigma = \epsilon_0 E_n$, where $E_n$ is the component of the electric field normal to the surface.

$$ E_n = E_z = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial z} \bigg|_{z=0} $$

After differentiation, we find the induced charge density on the plane is:

$$ \sigma(x,y) = -\frac{qd}{2\pi (x^2 + y^2 + d^2)^{3/2}} $$

The charge density is negative (as expected) and is strongest at the point on the plane directly below the charge $+q$.

Force on the Point Charge

The force experienced by the real charge $+q$ is simply the attractive Coulomb force exerted by the image charge $-q$.

$$ \vec{F} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{(q)(-q)}{(2d)^2} \hat{z} = -\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q^2}{4d^2} \hat{z} $$

The charge is pulled towards the conducting plane.

3. Point Charge and a Grounded Conducting Sphere

For a point charge $+q$ at a distance $d$ from the center of a grounded conducting sphere of radius $R$, the image charge required inside the sphere is more complex.

  • Magnitude of image charge: $q' = -q \frac{R}{d}$
  • Position of image charge: at a distance $b = \frac{R^2}{d}$ from the center, along the line connecting the center to the real charge $+q$.

This configuration of the real charge $q$ and the image charge $q'$ creates a potential of $V=0$ on the surface of the sphere, thus providing the correct solution for the potential outside the sphere.
