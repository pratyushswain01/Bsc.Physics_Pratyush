Introduction to Maxwell's Equations

Maxwell's equations are a set of four fundamental equations that, together with the Lorentz force law, form the complete foundation of classical electromagnetism, classical optics, and electric circuits. They describe how electric and magnetic fields are generated by charges and currents, and how they change in response to each other.

1. The Four Equations in Differential Form

The modern, local formulation of Maxwell's equations is expressed in differential form. Below are the equations as they apply in a vacuum.

I. Gauss's Law for Electricity

$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} $$

This law describes how electric charge creates an electric field. It states that the divergence of the electric field ($\vec{E}$) at any point is proportional to the volume charge density ($\rho$) at that point. In simple terms: electric charges are the sources of electric fields.

II. Gauss's Law for Magnetism

$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0 $$

This law states that the magnetic field ($\vec{B}$) has zero divergence. This means that there are no "magnetic charges" or magnetic monopoles. Magnetic field lines always form closed loops; they do not begin or end at any point.

III. Faraday's Law of Induction

$$ \nabla \times \vec{E} = - \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} $$

This law describes how a changing magnetic field creates a circulating electric field. This induced electric field is non-conservative (unlike the field from static charges) and is the mechanism behind electric generators and transformers.

IV. Ampere's Law with Maxwell's Addition

$$ \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} $$

Ampere's original law ($\nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J}$) was only valid for steady currents. Maxwell's crucial contribution was the addition of the second term, $\mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}$, known as the **displacement current**. This term states that a changing electric field also produces a circulating magnetic field, just as a real current does. This correction was necessary for theoretical consistency (specifically, for the conservation of charge) and led directly to the prediction of electromagnetic waves.

2. Maxwell's Equations and Electromagnetic Waves

The most profound consequence of Maxwell's equations is the prediction of self-sustaining electromagnetic waves that travel through a vacuum. In a region of free space with no charges or currents ($\rho=0, \vec{J}=0$), the equations become:

  1. $\nabla \cdot \vec{E} = 0$
  2. $\nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0$
  3. $\nabla \times \vec{E} = - \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}$
  4. $\nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}$

By taking the curl of equations III and IV, one can derive the wave equations for the electric and magnetic fields:

$$ \nabla^2 \vec{E} = \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \nabla^2 \vec{B} = \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \vec{B}}{\partial t^2} $$

These are the standard wave equations for waves traveling at a speed $c$. By comparing with the standard form $\nabla^2 f = \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial t^2}$, Maxwell predicted the speed of these waves to be:

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}} $$

Plugging in the known values for $\mu_0$ and $\epsilon_0$, the calculated speed was remarkably close to the experimentally measured speed of light. This was the first great unification in physics, proving that light is an electromagnetic wave.
