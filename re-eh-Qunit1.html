2 Marks Questions

1. Difference between renewable and non-renewable resources?

Renewable Resources are energy sources that replenish naturally over a short period. They are sustainable and generally have a low environmental impact. Examples: Solar, wind, hydro, and geothermal energy.

Non-Renewable Resources are finite resources that are consumed much faster than they are formed. Their extraction and use often cause significant environmental pollution. Examples: Fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) and nuclear fuel (uranium).

2. Explain about Energy and Energy harvesting.

Energy is the capacity to do work. It exists in various forms like kinetic, potential, thermal, and electromagnetic energy. In this context, it refers to the power we harness to run our societies.

Energy Harvesting (or energy scavenging) is the process of capturing minute amounts of energy from one or more ambient environmental sources (like light, heat, or vibrations) and converting it into usable electrical energy. It is crucial for powering low-energy electronics and sensors.

3. What do you mean by wind energy?

Wind energy is a form of solar energy. It describes the process by which wind is used to generate mechanical power or electricity. Wind turbines convert the kinetic energy of the moving air (wind) into mechanical power, which can then be used directly for tasks like grinding grain or, more commonly, converted into electricity by a generator.

The primary limitations of fossil fuels are:

5. How can renewable energy benefits the environment?

Renewable energy sources benefit the environment by producing little to no greenhouse gas emissions, reducing air and water pollution, conserving natural resources for future generations, and mitigating climate change. They also promote biodiversity by reducing the need for destructive extraction practices associated with fossil fuels.

6. Can renewable energy source replace fossil fuels?

Yes, renewable energy sources have the potential to largely replace fossil fuels. However, this transition faces challenges like intermittency (solar/wind are not always available), the need for massive energy storage solutions (like batteries), and the high initial cost of infrastructure. With ongoing technological advancements and investment, a full replacement is becoming increasingly feasible.

9. What are fossil fuels?

Fossil fuels are natural fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas, formed in the geological past from the remains of living organisms. They are carbon-based and have a high energy density, which is released when they are burned. They are the world's dominant energy source but are non-renewable and a primary driver of climate change.

10. Differentiate between conventional and nonconventional energy sources.

Conventional Sources are traditional, well-established, and widely used sources, which are typically non-renewable. Examples include fossil fuels (coal, oil) and large-scale hydropower.

Nonconventional Sources are newer, less common sources, which are typically renewable and more environmentally friendly. Examples include solar, wind, geothermal, tidal, and biomass energy.

8 Marks Questions

1. Give an account of solid fossil fuel. How is electricity generated from it?

The primary solid fossil fuel is coal. It is a combustible sedimentary rock formed over millions of years from compressed plant matter.

Electricity Generation from Coal:

  1. Pulverization: Raw coal is crushed into a fine powder to increase its surface area for efficient combustion.
  2. Combustion: The powdered coal is blown into a boiler furnace and burned at high temperatures, releasing a massive amount of thermal energy.
  3. Boiler and Steam Generation: This heat is used to boil water circulating in tubes within the boiler, producing high-pressure, high-temperature steam.
  4. Turbine Rotation: The high-pressure steam is directed at the blades of a turbine, causing it to rotate at high speeds. This converts thermal energy into mechanical energy.
  5. Generator: The turbine is connected to a generator, which contains coils of wire and magnets. As the turbine spins the generator, the changing magnetic field induces an electric current in the coils, generating electricity.
  6. Condensation and Cooling: After passing through the turbine, the low-pressure steam is cooled in a condenser (using water from a nearby source) back into liquid water, which is then pumped back to the boiler to repeat the cycle.

Wind Energy is the kinetic energy of moving air, which is harnessed and converted into useful forms of power, primarily electricity.

Key Concept: Wind is a form of solar energy. The sun's uneven heating of the atmosphere, the earth's irregularities, and its rotation all contribute to the creation of wind.

Advantages:

  • Clean Energy: Wind turbines produce no greenhouse gas emissions during operation.
  • Renewable: Wind is a natural and inexhaustible resource.
  • Cost-Effective: It is one of the lowest-priced renewable energy sources.
  • Land Use Efficiency: Wind farms can share land with agriculture or other uses.

Global Status:

Wind energy is one of the fastest-growing renewable energy technologies globally. Countries like China, the United States, Germany, and India are leaders in installed wind capacity. The industry is rapidly advancing, with larger, more efficient turbines and the development of offshore wind farms becoming increasingly common to harness stronger and more consistent winds.
