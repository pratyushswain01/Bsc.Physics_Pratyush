Multiple Choice Questions (1 Mark Each)

1. Piezoelectric effect is when materials produce electric charges when ___.

(b) Mechanical Stress is applied

Explanation: This is the definition of the direct piezoelectric effect.

2. Piezoelectricity means ___.

(c) Pressure electricity

Explanation: "Piezo" is from the Greek word for pressure or squeezing.

3. The piezoelectric materials used for converting energy are called as ___.

(d) Transducer

Explanation: A transducer converts one form of energy to another.

4. Piezoelectricity is shown by substances which are ___.

(d) Asymmetric

Explanation: The crystal structure must lack a center of symmetry for a net charge displacement to occur.

5. Piezoelectric effect is ___.

(b) Anisotropic

Explanation: The effect's magnitude depends on the direction of applied force relative to the crystal's axes.

6. Piezoelectric materials are ___.

(c) Both (a) and (b)

Explanation: Some (like quartz) are natural, while many high-performance ones (like PZT) are manufactured.

7. Piezoelectric transducer is used for measuring ___.

(a) non-electrical quantities

Explanation: It converts physical quantities (force, pressure) into a measurable electrical signal.

8. Piezoelectric crystals ___.

(b) dissolve in water

Explanation: This is true for some types like Rochelle salt, but not for ceramics like PZT or quartz.

9. Piezoelectric transducer consists of ___.

(d) quartz crystal

Explanation: Quartz is a classic natural material used in piezoelectric transducers.

10. Piezoelectric crystal produce ___.

(c) high voltage

Explanation: They are known for generating high voltage under stress, although with low current.

11. When a compressive force is applied to a quartz crystal then ___.

(a) positive charges are induced

Explanation: A compressive force induces charges of a certain polarity; tensile force induces the opposite.

12. In kitchen applications a piezoelectric crystal is used for ___.

(b) lighting a gas stove

Explanation: The "click" of a gas lighter is a piezoelectric igniter creating a spark.

13. A piezoelectric transducer has a ___.

(c) high sensitivity

Explanation: They are highly sensitive to small changes in pressure or force, which is why they are excellent sensors.

14. A piezoelectric transducer is used as an ignition source for a cigarette.

(a) True

Explanation: Similar to a gas stove lighter, many butane lighters use a piezoelectric sparker.

15. Microphone converts light into heat.

(b) False

Explanation: A microphone converts sound waves (mechanical energy) into an electrical signal.

16. A quartz crystal is ___.

(d) a self-generating transducer

Explanation: It is a passive or "self-generating" transducer because it generates its own electrical signal from an energy input without needing an external power source to operate.

17. The piezoelectric materials used for converting energy are called as ___.

(d) Transducer

Explanation: This is a repeat of Q3, confirming the role as an energy converter.

18. A piezoelectric transducer is a device that uses the piezoelectric effect to measure changes in ___.

(b) All of the above

Explanation: Acceleration and force both create stress, which in turn causes strain. The transducer measures the effect of these physical inputs.

19. Which of the following is not a piezoelectric material?

(c) Sodium Nitrate

Explanation: Lead Zirconate Titanate (PZT), Rochelle Salt, and Barium Titanate are all well-known piezoelectric materials.

20. Which of the following is not a disadvantage in Piezoelectric transducer?

(c) External voltage is required to produce output

Explanation: This is false, making it "not a disadvantage". Piezoelectric transducers are self-generating and do *not* require an external voltage.

21. Which of the following is not application of Piezoelectric transducer?

(d) In optical pyrometer

Explanation: An optical pyrometer measures temperature by sensing thermal radiation (light), a process unrelated to the piezoelectric effect.

22. The faces of piezoelectric materials are coated with ___ while using as transducers.

(c) Silver

Explanation: A conductive material, typically silver, is applied to the faces to act as electrodes for collecting the generated charge.

Short Questions and Answers

1. How can we harvest energy using piezoelectric?

Energy is harvested by strategically placing a piezoelectric transducer in an environment with ambient mechanical energy (like vibrations or impacts). As the material is repeatedly stressed or strained, it generates a continuous AC voltage. This electrical signal is then captured, rectified into DC, and stored in a capacitor or battery for later use.

2. Why do we harvest piezoelectric energy?

We harvest piezoelectric energy to create self-powered systems in places where batteries are inconvenient or impossible to replace. It offers a solution to convert otherwise wasted ambient energy into useful electricity for low-power applications like wireless sensors, medical implants, and remote electronics.

3. What causes the piezoelectric effect?

The effect is caused by the deformation of an asymmetric crystal lattice. When mechanical stress is applied, it displaces the centers of positive and negative charges within the crystal's unit cells. This creates a net electrical dipole moment, which, when summed over the entire crystal, produces a measurable voltage.

4. What is an energy harvesting system?

An energy harvesting system is a complete setup that scavenges ambient energy (like vibrations, light, or heat), converts it into electrical energy using a transducer, conditions that electrical signal (e.g., rectification and regulation), and stores it in an element like a capacitor or battery.

5. What is the role of piezoelectric material?

The primary role of a piezoelectric material is to act as the core transducer in an energy harvesting system. It performs the critical conversion of mechanical energy (stress/strain) into electrical energy (charge/voltage) via the direct piezoelectric effect.

6. How efficient are piezoelectric materials?

The efficiency of a complete harvesting system can be high, but it depends on many factors. The material's intrinsic efficiency is measured by its electromechanical coupling factor (k), which can be over 70% for materials like PZT. The overall system efficiency, after accounting for mechanical coupling and electrical power conditioning (like LDO regulators), can often reach over 80% under optimal, resonant conditions.

7. What is the use of a piezoelectric sensor?

A piezoelectric sensor is used to measure changes in physical parameters like pressure, acceleration, or force. The sensor converts the mechanical stress or strain caused by these parameters into a proportional electrical charge or voltage, which can then be easily measured and recorded by electronic instruments.

8. Where are piezoelectric materials used?

They are used in numerous fields: (i) Motors, (ii) Industrial Actuators, (iii) Medical Ultrasound, (iv) Consumer Electronics (speakers, buzzers), (v) Instrument Pickups (guitars), (vi) Microphones, (vii) Gas igniters, and (viii) a wide range of sensors.

9. How much power can a piezoelectric produce?

The power output is typically very low, in the range of nanowatts (nW) to milliwatts (mW). While a small device might produce up to 2mW from strong vibrations, this is generally enough to power low-energy electronics like wireless sensors or trickle-charge a small battery over time.

10. Why is energy harvesting used?

Energy harvesting is used to create autonomous, self-powered electronic systems. Its primary benefit is to power devices where conventional power sources (wires or batteries) are unavailable, impractical, or too expensive to maintain, such as in remote sensors or biomedical implants.

11. Why is energy harvesting important?

Its importance lies in its ability to enable the widespread deployment of the "Internet of Things" (IoT). By providing an alternative, "deploy-and-forget" power supply for billions of small, wireless devices, it eliminates the immense logistical challenge and cost of replacing batteries, making a truly smart and interconnected world feasible.

12. How are piezoelectric materials made?

Ceramic piezoelectric materials like PZT are made by mixing oxide powders, compacting them into a desired shape (pressing), and then firing them at a high temperature (sintering). A crucial final step is "poling," where a strong DC electric field is applied at a high temperature to align the crystal domains, inducing the piezoelectric properties in the ceramic.

13. Can piezoelectric charge a battery?

Yes. The alternating current from a piezoelectric transducer is first rectified to DC, then regulated by a power management circuit, and this conditioned power can be used to slowly trickle-charge a rechargeable battery or, more commonly, a supercapacitor, which is better suited for intermittent, low-power inputs.

14. How do you store piezoelectric energy?

After being generated and converted to a stable DC voltage, piezoelectric energy is typically stored in an electrical storage element. The most common choices are supercapacitors (for applications needing quick bursts of power) or small, rechargeable lithium-ion batteries (for applications requiring a steady, long-term power supply).

15. Is piezoelectric AC or DC?

The direct output from a piezoelectric material undergoing continuous vibration is an AC (alternating current) voltage. Compressing the material generates a voltage of one polarity, and when the force is removed (or it is stretched), a voltage of the opposite polarity is created.

16. How is piezoelectric voltage measured?

Piezoelectric voltage is typically measured using a high-impedance device like a charge amplifier or a high-input-impedance oscilloscope. This is necessary because the transducer itself has a very high internal impedance (like a small capacitor), and a standard low-impedance voltmeter would drain the charge too quickly to get an accurate reading.

17. What is piezoelectric pickup?

A piezoelectric pickup is a transducer used primarily in acoustic musical instruments (like guitars and violins). It consists of a piezoelectric crystal placed in contact with the instrument's body. The vibrations from the strings travel through the body to the crystal, which converts these mechanical vibrations directly into an electrical signal that can be amplified.

18. Can piezoelectric materials generate electricity?

Yes. When mechanically activated with pressure, stress, or vibration, piezoelectric materials generate a voltage. This effect is powerful enough in some devices (like gas lighters) to create a high-voltage spark across an air gap, demonstrating their ability to generate electricity directly from mechanical force.

19. Why is Silicon not piezoelectric?

Silicon is not piezoelectric because its crystal structure is **centrosymmetric**. This means it has a point of inversion symmetry. When stress is applied, the internal charge displacements occur, but because of the crystal's symmetry, they perfectly cancel each other out, leading to no net external voltage. The piezoelectric effect requires an asymmetric (non-centrosymmetric) lattice.

20. Where are piezoelectric transducers used?

They have numerous applications: they are used in **seismographs** to measure earth vibrations, in **strain gauges** to measure force, by automotive industries to **detonate engines** (knock sensors), and extensively in medical **ultrasonic imaging** to both generate and detect sound waves.

Descriptive Questions (5 Marks)

1. Describe the physics of the direct piezoelectric effect and why it occurs only in certain materials.

Physics of the Effect:

The direct piezoelectric effect is the generation of electrical charge in a material when mechanical stress is applied. This happens at the level of the crystal lattice. In specific crystalline materials, the arrangement of positive and negative ions forms a charge-neutral unit cell in its unstressed state.

  1. When an external force deforms this lattice, the positions of the ions shift.
  2. In a **non-centrosymmetric** crystal (one lacking a point of inversion symmetry), this shift separates the centers of positive and negative charge.
  3. This separation creates a small electric dipole in each unit cell.
  4. Across the millions of unit cells, these dipoles align and their effects sum up, producing a macroscopic voltage on the crystal's surface.
Why only certain materials? The key requirement is the lack of a center of symmetry. In a centrosymmetric crystal, any stress-induced charge displacement in one direction is perfectly canceled out by an equal and opposite displacement on the other side of the center point. Therefore, no net dipole moment is formed, and no voltage is generated.

2. What is the role of human power in piezoelectric energy harvesting? Give two practical application examples.

Human power serves as a convenient and ubiquitous source of mechanical energy for harvesting systems. Everyday human activities, like walking, typing, or even breathing, generate forces and vibrations that are normally wasted. Piezoelectric harvesting captures this kinetic energy to power personal devices and smart infrastructure, offering a sustainable source of low-level power.

Practical Application Examples:

  1. Smart Floors and Sidewalks: Piezoelectric transducers are installed under floor tiles in high-traffic areas (e.g., airports, train stations). The pressure from thousands of footsteps deforms the transducers, generating electricity. This harvested energy can power local systems like lighting, display screens, or environmental sensors.
  2. Self-Powered Wearable Devices: Transducers integrated into the heel of a shoe or fibers woven into clothing can convert the energy from walking or body movement into electricity. This can continuously trickle-charge a battery for a fitness tracker, a smartwatch, or a low-power medical sensor, extending its life and reducing charging frequency.

Long Answer Questions (8 Marks)

1. Provide a mathematical description of piezoelectricity and explain the key piezoelectric parameters.

Piezoelectricity is a coupled-field phenomenon where mechanical and electrical states are linked. This relationship is described by linear constitutive equations, which come in several forms depending on the variables used.

Mathematical Description:

The two most common forms of the constitutive equations are:

  1. Direct Effect (Sensor Form): This equation describes the electrical displacement (charge) generated in response to mechanical stress.
    D = dT + ε^T E
    Where:
    • D is the electric displacement vector (charge/area, C/m²).
    • d is the piezoelectric charge coefficient tensor (C/N).
    • T is the stress tensor (N/m²).
    • ε^T is the permittivity tensor under constant stress.
    • E is the electric field vector (V/m).
    This shows that the total charge on the material is a sum of the charge generated by stress (dT) and the charge stored due to an electric field (εE).
  2. Converse Effect (Actuator Form): This describes the mechanical strain (deformation) produced by an electric field.
    S = s^E T + d^t E
    Where:
    • S is the strain tensor (dimensionless).
    • s^E is the compliance tensor under a constant electric field.
    • d^t is the transpose of the piezoelectric charge coefficient tensor.
    This shows the total deformation is a sum of the normal elastic strain from stress (sT) and the strain induced by the electric field (dE).

Key Piezoelectric Parameters:

  • Piezoelectric Charge Coefficient (d): This is the most vital parameter for harvesters and sensors. It quantifies how much charge is generated per unit of applied force (units: Coulombs/Newton or C/N). A material with a high 'd' coefficient is excellent at converting force into charge.
  • Piezoelectric Voltage Coefficient (g): This quantifies the electric field produced per unit of applied stress (units: Volt-meters/Newton or Vm/N). Materials with a high 'g' coefficient are good at generating a high voltage signal, which is useful for sensors. It is related to 'd' by the equation g = d/ε.
  • Electromechanical Coupling Factor (k): This dimensionless parameter measures the efficiency of the energy conversion process. It represents the ratio of mechanical energy converted into electrical energy (or vice versa) to the total input energy. A k value of 1 signifies perfect conversion. For high-quality materials like PZT, k is often between 0.5 and 0.75, indicating high conversion efficiency.

2. Design a conceptual model for a piezoelectric generator. Discuss material selection and modeling considerations.

Conceptual Model: A Resonant Cantilever Beam Harvester

This design is highly effective for scavenging energy from predictable, steady-state vibrations found on industrial machinery, vehicles, and infrastructure.

Design & Components:

  1. Structure: The core is a flexible **cantilever beam** (like a small diving board), clamped firmly at one end.
  2. Piezoelectric Layer: A thin layer of a piezoelectric ceramic is bonded onto the surface of the beam near the clamped end, where the bending stress is highest.
  3. Tip Mass: A small but significant mass is attached to the free end of the cantilever. This mass is crucial for tuning the system's resonant frequency.
  4. Electrodes: Conductive metal layers (e.g., silver) are placed on the top and bottom faces of the piezoelectric layer to collect the generated electrical charge.

Working Principle:

  • The generator's clamped base is attached to a vibrating source.
  • The vibration causes the base to move up and down. The inertia of the tip mass resists this motion, causing the beam to bend and flex significantly.
  • This bending action induces alternating cycles of tensile and compressive stress in the piezoelectric layer.
  • Each cycle of stress generates an AC voltage across the electrodes. This signal is then sent to a power management circuit for rectification and storage.
The key to high efficiency is resonance. The dimensions of the beam and the size of the tip mass are carefully calculated so that the beam's natural mechanical frequency matches the source's vibration frequency. This amplifies the bending motion, maximizing stress and power output.

Material Selection:

  • Piezoelectric Material: Lead Zirconate Titanate (PZT) ceramic is the material of choice due to its exceptionally high piezoelectric charge coefficient (d) and electromechanical coupling factor (k), leading to the highest power output for a given size.
  • Beam Substrate: The cantilever itself should be a durable and elastic material like **spring steel**, **brass**, or **silicon** to withstand millions of bending cycles without fatigue.

Modeling Considerations:

  • Mechanical Modeling: The system is first modeled as a damped, driven harmonic oscillator to determine its resonant frequency. Advanced Finite Element Analysis (FEA) software is then used to simulate stress distribution along the beam, predict power output, and optimize the geometry (shape and size) of the beam and tip mass.
  • Electrical Modeling: The piezoelectric element is modeled as a current source in parallel with an internal capacitance. The entire electrical circuit, including the load impedance, must be modeled to ensure proper **impedance matching**, a critical step for transferring the maximum amount of power from the harvester to the storage element.
