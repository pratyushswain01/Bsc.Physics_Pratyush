Electrostatic Potential & Electric Dipole

While the electric field describes the force on a charge, electrostatic potential describes the energy associated with its position. This section introduces the concept of potential, its relationship to the electric field, and its application to a fundamental charge configuration: the electric dipole.

1. Electrostatic Potential (V)

Electrostatic potential at a point in an electric field is defined as the work done by an external agent in moving a unit positive charge from a reference point (usually infinity) to that point, without acceleration.

$$ V(\vec{r}) = \frac{W_{\infty \to r}}{q_0} $$

Since the electrostatic force is conservative, the work done is path-independent. This allows us to define a scalar potential field. The relationship between potential and potential energy ($U$) is simply $U = qV$. The SI unit of potential is the Volt (V), where 1 Volt = 1 Joule/Coulomb.

Relationship with Electric Field

The potential difference between two points A and B is the negative line integral of the electric field:

$$ \Delta V = V_B - V_A = -\int_A^B \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} $$

Conversely, the electric field is the negative gradient of the potential. This is a crucial relationship that allows calculating one from the other.

$$ \vec{E} = -\nabla V $$

This means the electric field points in the direction of the steepest decrease in potential.

Potential due to a Point Charge

By integrating the electric field of a point charge from infinity, we find the potential at a distance $r$ from a charge $q$ is:

$$ V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r} $$

Note that potential is a scalar quantity, which makes calculations involving multiple charges much simpler than for the vector electric field. For a system of charges, the total potential is the algebraic sum of the individual potentials:

$$ V_{\text{total}} = \sum_i V_i = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \sum_i \frac{q_i}{r_i} $$

2. The Electric Dipole

An electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite point charges, $+q$ and $-q$, separated by a small vector distance $\vec{d}$.

Dipole Moment

The strength and orientation of a dipole are characterized by its electric dipole moment, $\vec{p}$, a vector quantity defined as:

$$ \vec{p} = q\vec{d} $$

The vector $\vec{d}$ points from the negative charge to the positive charge.

Potential due to a Dipole

The potential at a point P, located by a position vector $\vec{r}$ far from the dipole ($r \gg d$), can be found by summing the potentials of the two charges. Using geometric approximations, this simplifies to:

$$ V(\vec{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{p \cos\theta}{r^2} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{\vec{p} \cdot \hat{r}}{r^2} $$

Here, $\theta$ is the angle between the dipole moment vector $\vec{p}$ and the position vector $\vec{r}$.

Electric Field of a Dipole

The electric field of a dipole can be calculated by taking the negative gradient of its potential ($E = -\nabla V$) in spherical coordinates. The components are:

  • Radial component: $E_r = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial r} = \frac{2p \cos\theta}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^3}$
  • Tangential component: $E_\theta = -\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial V}{\partial \theta} = \frac{p \sin\theta}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^3}$

Notice that the field of a dipole falls off as $1/r^3$, which is faster than the $1/r^2$ decay of a single point charge.

3. Dipole in an External Electric Field

Torque on a Dipole

When a dipole is placed in a uniform external electric field $\vec{E}$, the forces on the two charges form a couple, resulting in a torque that tends to align the dipole with the field. The torque is given by:

$$ \vec{\tau} = \vec{p} \times \vec{E} $$

Potential Energy of a Dipole

The potential energy $U$ of a dipole in an external field is the work done to rotate it from a position of zero energy (usually $\theta = 90^\circ$) to its current orientation. It is given by the dot product:

$$ U = - \vec{p} \cdot \vec{E} $$
  • The energy is minimum ($U = -pE$) when the dipole is aligned with the field ($\theta = 0^\circ$), which is a position of stable equilibrium.
  • The energy is maximum ($U = +pE$) when the dipole is anti-aligned with the field ($\theta = 180^\circ$), which is a position of unstable equilibrium.
